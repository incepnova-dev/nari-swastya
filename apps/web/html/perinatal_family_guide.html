Section 3 — Nutrition & Wellness

Evidence-Based Nutrition & Wellness

Targeted nutritional support and lifestyle interventions that directly impact brain chemistry and mood regulation. Based on ACOG and ICMR clinical guidelines.

Critical Nutrients

  • Omega-3 EPA >1000mg/day: Improves neurotransmitter function and reduces inflammation
  • Vitamin D3: Target serum levels 40–60 ng/mL — deficiency linked to 2× PPD risk
  • Magnesium (300–400mg): Reduces anxiety, aids deep sleep, supports HPA axis
  • Folate & B12: Critical for serotonin and dopamine synthesis pathways
  • Iron: Anaemia affects over 50% of Indian postpartum women (NFHS-5) — directly worsens fatigue and mood

Daily Support Rituals

  • Protein + Complex Carbs every 3–4 hours: Stabilizes blood sugar & serotonin
  • 2.5–3L hydration/day: Combats fatigue, headaches & milk supply
  • 10–15 min sunlight: Boosts Vitamin D & resets circadian rhythm
  • 20-min walk 3×/week: Exercise reduces PPD severity by 26% (Cochrane, 2023)
  • Fermented foods (curd/yogurt): Gut–brain axis plays key role in mood regulation

Sleep Hygiene

  • Partners: Take the 2 AM feeding shift — protecting one 4-hour sleep block is life-changing
  • Sleep when baby sleeps: do not use this time for chores
  • Reduce evening blue-light exposure 90 mins before bed
  • Dark, cool room (18–20°C): Optimal sleep temperature per sleep science
  • Melatonin 0.5mg if needed — safe in postpartum (consult OB first)

Section 4 — Disease Trajectory

Understanding the Timeline

Understanding the natural progression helps identify intervention points. Baby blues vs. depression vs. psychosis: knowing the difference saves lives.

DAYS 3–10 POSTPARTUM

Baby Blues

Affects 50–80% of mothers (WHO). Tearfulness, mood swings, mild anxiety. Typically self-limiting within 2 weeks. Caused by rapid hormonal shifts. No treatment needed — just support and rest.

2+ WEEKS POSTPARTUM

Perinatal Depression Onset

Symptoms persist beyond the "blues" window. Interferes with daily functioning, bonding, and self-care. EPDS score ≥10 indicates likely depression. Professional evaluation required.

ANYTIME (RARE BUT SERIOUS)

Postpartum Anxiety Disorder

Constant worry, panic attacks, hypervigilance about baby's safety. Often overlooked but affects 15–20% (ACOG). May coexist with depression. Respond with CBT + therapy.

EMERGENCY — ACT IMMEDIATELY

Postpartum Psychosis

Rare (1–2 per 1000 births) but psychiatric emergency. Hallucinations, delusions, rapid mood shifts, confusion, detachment from reality. Call emergency services immediately. Do not leave her alone.

When to Act

When to Consult a Provider

Clear urgency-level guidelines ensure timely intervention. When in doubt — act immediately.

IMMEDIATE (Within 24 Hours / Call Emergency)
  • Suicidal thoughts, statements, or plan
  • Thoughts of harming the baby or other children
  • Hallucinations (seeing or hearing things not there)
  • Severe confusion, paranoia, or loss of touch with reality
  • Refusing to eat for 24+ hours
URGENT (Within 48–72 Hours)
  • Worsening symptoms affecting ability to care for baby
  • Severe insomnia not related to baby's schedule
  • Recurrent panic attacks
  • Complete inability to bond with the baby
  • Extreme rage episodes disproportionate to situation
ROUTINE (Schedule Appointment This Week)
  • Persistent low mood lasting more than 2 weeks
  • Difficulty bonding with the baby
  • Feeling overwhelmed constantly despite adequate help
  • Loss of interest in things she previously enjoyed
  • EPDS score of 10 or above

Section 7 — Myth Busters

Bust the Myths. Know the Truth.

Every myth below has kept a real woman from getting help. Tap each card, learn the truth — then share it with the women you love. You could change everything.

1
MYTH
"Perinatal depression is just 'baby blues' — it'll pass on its own."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Baby blues (days 3–10) resolve naturally. But clinical perinatal depression is a diagnosable medical condition lasting months without treatment. It involves dysregulation of serotonin, dopamine, and the HPA axis. Treatment (therapy, medication) is highly effective — 80% of women recover fully with appropriate care.
ACOG, 2023
2
MYTH
"Only new mothers get postpartum depression."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
4–10% of fathers and partners also experience perinatal depression. Adoptive parents, grandparents, and those experiencing pregnancy loss also develop perinatal mood disorders. Depression can begin during pregnancy (antenatal depression) — not just after birth.
JAMA, 2023
3
MYTH
"Antidepressants during breastfeeding will harm the baby."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Multiple SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine) are considered compatible with breastfeeding by LactMed, ACOG, and WHO. Transfer to breast milk is minimal. The risk of untreated severe depression far outweighs the small theoretical risk from medication. Always consult a perinatal psychiatrist.
WHO · ACOG · LactMed
4
MYTH
"If she loved her baby, she wouldn't be depressed."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Perinatal depression is a biological illness — not a reflection of love, character, or willpower. It is caused by hormonal shifts, neuroinflammation, sleep deprivation, and genetic vulnerability. A mother can love her baby deeply and still be seriously ill. This myth delays help-seeking by 6–12 months on average.
Lancet Psychiatry, 2021
5
MYTH
"She won't get better — this is just how she is now."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
With appropriate treatment, 80%+ of women with perinatal depression fully recover. Both psychotherapy (CBT, IPT) and pharmacotherapy are highly effective. Recovery typically takes 6 months to a year — shorter with early intervention and strong partner support. Hope is not just warranted — it is evidence-based.
WHO, 2023
6
MYTH
"Asking for help means you're a bad mother."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Asking for help is one of the bravest, most selfless things a mother can do. Untreated depression affects the baby's emotional development and bonding. Mothers who seek treatment recover faster and build stronger bonds with their children. Seeking help IS good mothering.
Postpartum Support International
7
MYTH
"Perinatal depression only happens during pregnancy, not after."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Perinatal depression spans the entire perinatal period — from conception through the first year postpartum. Antenatal depression (during pregnancy) affects 10–15% of women and is just as serious. Many cases go unrecognised because symptoms overlap with normal pregnancy experiences.
NIMH · RCOG
8
MYTH
"Strong women don't get depressed — she just needs to toughen up."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Perinatal depression affects 1 in 7 women regardless of strength, resilience, or personality. It is triggered by hormonal disruption, neurobiological changes, and social stressors — not weakness. Many of the strongest women in history have experienced depression. Recognising this is true strength.
APA, 2022
9
MYTH
"If she had thoughts of harming her baby, she's dangerous and should lose custody."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Intrusive thoughts — unwanted, distressing thoughts that the mother finds horrifying — are a common symptom of perinatal OCD and anxiety, affecting up to 57% of new mothers. They are NOT a sign of intent or danger. They indicate a woman who needs compassionate psychiatric care, not punishment. Disclosing these thoughts is an act of courage.
Fairbrother & Woody, 2021 · MGH
10
MYTH
"Depression during pregnancy is rare — it mostly happens after birth."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Antenatal depression is actually more common than postpartum depression, affecting up to 20% of pregnant women globally. It is frequently missed because symptoms (fatigue, sleep changes, appetite shifts) overlap with normal pregnancy. Screening during all antenatal appointments is recommended by ACOG and NICE guidelines.
ACOG · NICE Guidelines
11
MYTH
"Therapy is unnecessary — she just needs more support from family."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Family support is vital — but it cannot replace professional treatment. Clinical depression involves measurable neurobiological changes that require evidence-based intervention. CBT and Interpersonal Therapy (IPT) have been shown to be as effective as medication for mild-to-moderate perinatal depression. Both support AND professional care are needed.
Cochrane Review, 2022
12
MYTH
"She smiled today — she must be fine. She was faking it."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Smiling depression is real and dangerous. Many women with severe perinatal depression appear functional in public while suffering intensely in private. This "high-functioning" presentation is especially common in women with strong social obligations or fear of stigma. One good day does not mean recovery — consistent professional follow-up is essential.
Journal of Affective Disorders, 2022
13
MYTH
"Perinatal depression doesn't affect the baby — only the mother suffers."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Untreated perinatal depression significantly impacts infant outcomes. Babies of depressed mothers show disrupted attachment, delayed cognitive development, increased cortisol (stress hormone) levels, and higher rates of behavioural problems in childhood. Treating the mother's depression is among the most powerful investments in the baby's lifelong health.
Field, T. — Infant Behaviour, 2022
14
MYTH
"It's normal to feel this way — every new mother feels exhausted and overwhelmed."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Normal new-mother exhaustion is real — but it is different from clinical depression. Key differences: clinical depression involves persistent hopelessness, loss of joy in everything (not just tiredness), inability to bond with baby, intrusive thoughts, inability to function, or thoughts of self-harm. When in doubt, always screen with the EPDS. Normalising suffering delays diagnosis.
Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale — WHO
15
MYTH
"She had a planned pregnancy and a healthy baby — she has no reason to be depressed."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Depression does not require a "reason." Perinatal depression is triggered by biology — specifically the dramatic drop in oestrogen and progesterone after birth, combined with sleep deprivation and neurobiological changes. Even women with perfect pregnancies, loving partners, and wanted babies develop severe perinatal depression. External circumstances do not determine brain chemistry.
NIMH · Harvard Medical School
16
MYTH
"Perinatal depression isn't a real illness — it's just a lifestyle label."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Perinatal depression is classified in DSM-5 and ICD-11 as a specifier of Major Depressive Disorder. Brain imaging studies show measurable changes in the amygdala, prefrontal cortex, and hippocampus. It has a documented 10–15% prevalence globally and is the leading cause of maternal mortality in high-income countries through suicide. It is unambiguously real.
DSM-5 · ICD-11 · WHO
17
MYTH
"Once you have perinatal depression, you'll have it in every pregnancy."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
While women with a history of perinatal depression do have a higher risk of recurrence (about 50%), this is not inevitable. With proactive screening, early intervention, and social support planning, many women have subsequent pregnancies without depression. Knowing the risk allows for prevention — it is not a life sentence.
MGH Center for Women's Mental Health
18
MYTH
"Talking about it will make it worse — it's better to stay positive."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Research consistently shows that emotional suppression worsens depression. Talking — especially in structured therapy — reduces symptom severity, helps process trauma, and builds resilience. Forcing positivity invalidates her experience and increases shame. Saying "I'm here, tell me what you're feeling" is far more healing than "think positive."
Pennebaker, JW — Clinical Psychology Review
19
MYTH
"Yoga, diet and self-care will cure perinatal depression."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Exercise, nutrition, omega-3s, and sleep are evidence-supported *adjuncts* to treatment — not replacements. For mild depression they can help meaningfully. For moderate-to-severe perinatal depression, they are insufficient alone. Delaying medication or therapy in favour of lifestyle changes in severe cases increases risk of chronicity and maternal suicide.
BJOG · Cochrane Review, 2023
20
MYTH
"She should be over it by now — it's been months."
Tap to reveal the truth
REALITY
Untreated perinatal depression can last 1–2 years or longer and can evolve into chronic major depressive disorder. Even with treatment, recovery is not linear — there are setbacks. The average time from onset to treatment in India is 18 months due to stigma and lack of awareness. Patience and consistent support are not optional — they are medically essential.
ICMR National Mental Health Survey, 2022

Section 9 — EPDS Screening

Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale

The EPDS is the most widely validated screening tool for perinatal depression worldwide — used by WHO, ACOG, and ICMR. Understanding the scoring helps you advocate effectively.

Understanding the EPDS

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale consists of 10 questions assessing mood, anxiety, and self-harm risk over the past 7 days. Each item is scored 0–3, giving a total of 0–30.

It was developed in 1987 by Cox, Holden & Sagovsky and is now recommended as standard screening by ACOG (first postpartum visit), WHO (all perinatal contacts), and ICMR (integrated into national maternal health programs).

  • 0–9:   Low risk — routine support
  • 10–12:   Mild — close monitoring + counseling
  • 13–19:   Moderate-Severe — immediate referral
  • 20+:   Severe — psychiatric emergency referral
  • Q10 ≥1:   Any score — immediate safety assessment

Key Point for Partners: If her score is ≥10, accompany her to a healthcare provider. If Question 10 scores ≥1, contact a crisis resource immediately regardless of total score.

Section 11 — Hope

Your Support Saves Lives

Recovery from perinatal depression takes time — typically 6 months to a year with treatment. There will be good days and difficult days. Your patience, understanding, and active support are essential to her recovery. Research confirms: strong partner support cuts recovery time by up to 40%.

"She is not weak. She is not a bad mother. She is ill. And with proper treatment and support, she WILL recover. Your love and advocacy make an immeasurable difference."

You don't need to be perfect. You just need to be present, compassionate, and willing to help. That is enough — and it is everything.

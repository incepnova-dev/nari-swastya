Prenatal vs Postnatal Depression

Understanding the distinct hormonal, physical, and emotional changes in each phase

Prenatal Depression

During Pregnancy

Hormonal Changes

  • Progesterone surge: Increases 10-fold, causes fatigue and mood sensitivity
  • Estrogen rise: Multiplies 100-fold by third trimester, affects serotonin
  • hCG elevation: Triggers nausea, impacts mood regulation
  • Cortisol increase: Stress response heightened throughout pregnancy

Physical Changes

  • Rapid body transformation, weight gain, physical discomfort
  • Morning sickness, fatigue, sleep disturbances
  • Increased blood volume, cardiovascular changes
  • Breast tenderness, pelvic pressure, back pain

Emotional Patterns

  • Anxiety about baby's health and labor/delivery
  • Fear of inadequacy as a future mother
  • Mood swings from hormonal fluctuations
  • Concerns about relationship changes and identity shift

Postnatal Depression

After Childbirth

Hormonal Changes

  • Estrogen crash: 100-fold drop within 24 hours postpartum
  • Progesterone plunge: Rapid decline disrupts GABA signaling
  • Oxytocin irregularity: Fluctuates with breastfeeding, affects bonding
  • Thyroid dysfunction: 5-10% develop postpartum thyroiditis

Physical Changes

  • Severe sleep deprivation (averaging 2-4 hours nightly)
  • Physical recovery from childbirth, perineal/cesarean healing
  • Breastfeeding challenges, nipple pain, engorgement
  • Postpartum bleeding, cramping, physical exhaustion

Emotional Patterns

  • Overwhelming responsibility for newborn's survival
  • Guilt about lack of bonding or maternal feelings
  • Loss of identity, autonomy, and personal time
  • Social isolation, reduced partner intimacy
Edinburgh Scale · EPDS · DSM-5

Symptom Recognition by Severity

Understanding the progression from mild to critical symptoms enables early intervention and appropriate care. Click each card to explore symptoms in detail.

Mild Symptoms

Edinburgh Score 10-12
  • Tearfulness occurring 3-4 times weekly, particularly evening hours
  • Sleep disturbance beyond infant care needs (difficulty falling asleep after 30+ minutes)
  • Reduced enjoyment in previously pleasurable activities, appetite changes ±10%
  • Intrusive worries about infant health/safety occurring hourly but controllable
  • Mild irritability with partner/family, guilt feelings about motherhood competence

Moderate Symptoms

Edinburgh Score 13-15
  • Persistent low mood lasting 2+ weeks, daily tearfulness regardless of circumstances
  • Early morning wakening (3-4 AM) with inability to return to sleep, total sleep <4 hours/night
  • Significant appetite loss or emotional overeating, weight change >5% in 2 weeks
  • Concentration impairment affecting infant care tasks (forgetting feeding times)
  • Pronounced guilt about not bonding with baby, feeling like a bad mother constantly
  • Social withdrawal (avoiding friends, declining support offers), reduced self-care

Critical Symptoms

Edinburgh Score 16-19
  • Pervasive anhedonia (inability to feel joy even during positive infant interactions)
  • Psychomotor retardation (slowed movements, speech) or agitation (constant restlessness)
  • Cognitive dulling (decision paralysis about simple tasks, difficulty following conversations)
  • Intrusive thoughts of infant harm (not acted upon but distressing, recurring multiple times daily)
  • Feelings of worthlessness, excessive guilt, belief that baby would be better off without mother
  • Impaired infant care (delayed response to crying, minimal interaction)

Serious/Suicidal

Edinburgh Score 20+
  • ANY suicidal ideation: passive (wish to not wake up) or active (specific plan/intent)
  • Psychotic features: auditory hallucinations commanding harm to self/infant, delusions
  • Complete emotional detachment from infant (no response to distress cues, absent eye contact)
  • Severe agitation or catatonic states, inability to perform basic infant care tasks
  • Infanticidal thoughts (recurrent, ego-dystonic thoughts of harming infant)

Brain Chemistry & Hormonal Changes

Perinatal depression involves complex interactions between neurotransmitters and hormones that regulate mood, bonding, stress response, and emotional well-being. Hover over each card to see the impact.

Serotonin

Mood Regulator & Emotional Stabilizer

Essential for mood stability, sleep regulation, and appetite control. Low serotonin levels are strongly associated with depressive symptoms, anxiety, and obsessive thoughts about infant care. Estrogen withdrawal postpartum directly reduces serotonin receptor sensitivity.

Typical Depression Level
40%

Oxytocin

Bonding Hormone & Social Connection

Released during breastfeeding and skin-to-skin contact, oxytocin facilitates mother-infant bonding. Reduced oxytocin signaling impairs attachment and increases feelings of detachment from baby. Stress and depression can suppress oxytocin release.

Impaired Bonding Level
45%

Cortisol

Stress Hormone & HPA Axis Dysregulation

Chronically elevated cortisol from sleep deprivation and postpartum stress suppresses serotonin production. Dysregulated cortisol rhythm contributes to anxiety, hypervigilance, exhaustion, and impaired immune function during the perinatal period.

Elevated Stress Level
85%

GABA

Calming Neurotransmitter & Anxiety Reducer

The primary inhibitory neurotransmitter that reduces neuronal excitability. Low GABA activity contributes to anxiety, panic attacks, and intrusive thoughts common in perinatal depression. Progesterone withdrawal postpartum disrupts GABA receptor function.

Reduced Calming Activity
35%

Dopamine

Motivation & Reward System

Drives motivation, pleasure, and reward processing. Dopamine dysfunction leads to anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure), lack of motivation for infant care, reduced interest in activities, and emotional flatness characteristic of severe perinatal depression.

Depleted Motivation
30%

Estrogen

Neuroplasticity & Serotonin Modulation

Estrogen levels plummet dramatically after delivery (100-fold decrease within 24 hours). This rapid withdrawal affects serotonin receptors, GABA signaling, and overall mood regulation, triggering depressive symptoms. Estrogen also influences brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF).

Postpartum Decline
20%

Progesterone

Calming Hormone & GABA Modulator

Progesterone's metabolite allopregnanolone acts as a powerful GABA-A receptor modulator, producing calming effects. The rapid postpartum drop in progesterone disrupts this system, contributing to anxiety, insomnia, and mood instability in susceptible women.

Postpartum Crash
25%

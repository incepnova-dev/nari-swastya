🧠 The Brain: Thermostat Glitch

Fluctuating estrogen confuses the hypothalamus, triggering hot flashes. You may experience "brain fog" as neurons adjust to new fuel sources.

🌡️ Thermoregulation Affected
🧩 Cognitive Adaptation Phase
❤️ The Heart: Palpitations

Erratic hormones can cause benign but scary heart palpitations. Blood vessels begin to lose some elasticity.

💓 Rhythm Changes Common
🩺 Monitor Blood Pressure
🌸 Reproductive: The Storm

Cycles shorten or skip. Heavy bleeding is common due to progesterone dropping faster than estrogen (estrogen dominance).

📊 Cycle: 21-45 days
💧 Flow: Variable/Heavy
🦴 Skeleton: Silent Loss

Bone breakdown (resorption) begins to outpace building. This is the start of the "danger zone" for density loss.

⚠️ Loss Rate: 1-2% per year
💪 Action: Start resistance training
✨ Skin: Sensitivity

As hormones fluctuate, you may notice drier skin, new sensitivity to products, or adult acne. Collagen production begins to slow.

💧 Hydration Critical
🧴 Collagen: Declining
🧠 The Brain: Adaptation Phase

Hot flashes peak during this phase. The brain is actively rewiring to function with lower estrogen levels. Sleep disruption is very common.

🔥 Hot Flashes: Peak Intensity
😴 Sleep: Often Disrupted
❤️ The Heart: Critical Transition

Cardiovascular changes accelerate without estrogen's protective effects. LDL cholesterol often rises. This is a critical prevention window.

📈 LDL: Rising
🏃‍♀️ Exercise: Essential
🌸 Reproductive: Final Cycles

Last menstrual periods occurring. The uterine lining becomes much thinner. Fat distribution begins shifting from hips to abdomen.

⏰ Final Period: Within 12 months
📍 Fat Shift: Hips → Belly
🦴 Skeleton: Accelerated Loss

Rapid bone density decline begins. The first 2-3 years after final period are the most critical for prevention interventions.

🚨 Loss Rate: 3-5% per year
🥛 Calcium + Vit D Essential
✨ Skin: Visible Changes

Collagen loss becomes noticeable - skin appears thinner. Skin thickness actually decreases. Maintaining hydration becomes more challenging.

📉 Collagen: -30% in 5 years
💦 Moisture Barrier: Weakened
🧠 The Brain: New Baseline

Hot flashes typically subside for most women. The brain has successfully adapted to functioning with lower estrogen. Alzheimer's risk slightly elevated without estrogen's neuroprotective effects.

✅ Adaptation: Complete
🧘‍♀️ Cognitive Training Helpful
❤️ The Heart: Metabolic Shift Complete

Without estrogen's protection, LDL often rises and arterial plaque can build faster. Regular monitoring of blood pressure and lipid panels is critical now.

⚕️ CVD Risk: Increased
📊 Annual Lipid Panel Required
🌸 Metabolism: "Menopause Belly"

Fat storage has shifted from subcutaneous (hips/thighs) to visceral (abdomen). This is not just cosmetic - visceral fat is metabolically active and inflammatory.

⚠️ Visceral Fat: Inflammatory
🏋️‍♀️ Strength Training Critical
🦴 Skeleton: Osteopenia/Osteoporosis Risk

Rapid density loss continues in first 5 years post-menopause. Trabecular bone (the spongy inner bone) becomes significantly thinner and more brittle.

🚨 DEXA Scan: Every 2 years
🦴 Fracture Risk: Elevated
✨ Skin: Structural Change

Collagen levels can drop by 30% in the first 5 years post-menopause. Skin becomes noticeably thinner and more prone to bruising or tearing. Deep hydration is key.

📉 Thickness: -1.1% per year
🧴 Retinoids + Peptides Help

Domain 1 of 8

Menopausal Hormone Therapy

MHT remains the single most effective treatment for vasomotor symptoms and is strongly recommended for eligible women within the window of opportunity.

NAMS 2022 HT Position Statement
IMS White Paper 2024
FIGO Int'l J Gyn Obstet 2024
ACOG Clinical Guidance
🔑
Key Counseling Point — FIGO 2024 "Healthcare practitioners must protect the health and lives of mid-life women by increasing awareness of menopausal symptoms and ensuring healthcare options, especially MHT — counseling is a fundamental part of peri- and postmenopausal care."
Who Benefits Most
Benefit-to-risk ratio is favourable for healthy women under 60, or within 10 years of menopause onset, with no absolute contraindications. MHT reduces VMS, prevents bone loss, and reduces fracture risk.
Route & Formulation
Transdermal 17β-estradiol + micronised progesterone is preferred for most women — carries lower breast cancer and VTE signal vs. synthetic progestogens. Transdermal estrogen has lower VTE risk than oral routes.
Uterus-Specific Guidance
Women with a uterus must receive adequate progestogen to protect the endometrium. Women without a uterus can use estrogen alone. Type, dose, and route should be individually tailored at shared review.
Duration of Use
Duration should not be arbitrarily limited — persistent, bothersome symptoms are a valid indication for continued use with annual shared review of benefits and risks. No mandatory stopping age.
Later Initiation (>60 yrs / >10 yrs)
Women aged >60 or >10 years from FMP initiating MHT for the first time require more careful risk-benefit counseling; de novo initiation carries a less favourable cardiovascular and breast cancer profile.
Shared Decision-Making
All counseling must be individualised. Prescriber should document discussion of VTE, breast cancer, cardiovascular, and bone outcomes specific to formulation, route, dose, and patient risk factors.
📖 NAMS 2022 HT Position Statement 📖 IMS 2024 White Paper 📖 FIGO Int'l J Gyn Obstet 2024
🧠
Domain 2 of 8

CBT & Psychological Counseling

NICE 2024 (NG23) formally recommends menopause-specific CBT as a primary management option — not merely a fallback. Discuss alongside MHT, not only when MHT is declined.

NICE NG23 (Updated Nov 2024)
NAMS 2023 Non-Hormone Statement
ICMR Consensus Guidelines
🔑
Counseling Action — NICE NG23, 2024 Explain what CBT is, including menopause-specific CBT, discuss all available delivery formats (individual, group, self-help, digital), and take the person's preferences and needs into account.
CBT for VMS
Face-to-face, group, remote, or self-guided formats are all evidence-supported for vasomotor symptoms. Clinician should discuss all modalities — delivery format matters and should match patient preference.
CBT for Sleep
Recommended specifically for night-time awakening associated with VMS — either as adjunct to MHT or as primary treatment when MHT is contraindicated or declined (NICE 2024 Recommendation 1.7).
CBT for Low Mood
Consider CBT where symptoms do not meet clinical threshold for a diagnosis of depression, alongside MHT if appropriate. For diagnosed depression, follow concurrent depression guidelines concurrently.
Mindfulness (MBSR)
Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction has emerging evidence for mood, sleep, and hot flash bother. NAMS 2023 lists it as a recommended non-pharmacological approach with a low harm profile.
India-Specific Unmet Need
ICMR notes psychological support is severely underutilised in Indian women due to cultural stigma around menopause. Counseling must address sociocultural framing of menopause as loss vs. natural transition.
Clinical Hypnosis
NAMS 2023 includes clinical hypnosis as having Level I evidence for hot flash bother reduction and Level II evidence for mood and sleep improvement — routinely offer alongside CBT.
📖 NICE NG23 Nov 2024 📖 NAMS 2023 Non-Hormone Position Statement 📖 ICMR Consensus

Domain 3 of 8

GSM & Sexual Health

Unlike VMS, GSM is progressive and does not resolve without treatment. Clinicians must routinely ask — women often do not volunteer symptoms due to embarrassment.

AUA/SUFU/AUGS Guideline 2025
ICSM 2024 Consensus
NICE NG23 2024
NAMS 2020 GSM Position Statement
🔑
Key Counseling Message — AUA/SUFU/AUGS 2025 "Shared decision-making is the optimal strategy — clinicians should educate, empower, and dispel myths about GSM, emphasising that treatment is safe and effective and that untreated GSM worsens quality of life progressively."
First-Line: All Women
Counsel on regular use of non-hormonal vaginal moisturisers (polyacrylic acid, hyaluronic acid) and silicone or water-based lubricants for dyspareunia. No prescription required — can be started immediately.
Moderate-to-Severe GSM
Offer low-dose vaginal estrogen (cream, gel, pessary, tablet, or ring). A systemic progestogen is NOT required as serum absorption is minimal (NICE 2024, AUA 2025). Review at 3 months, then annually.
DHEA & Ospemifene
Vaginal DHEA (prasterone) and ospemifene (a SERM) are effective, guideline-endorsed alternatives where vaginal estrogen is declined or not tolerated (NAMS 2020, AUA 2025).
Recurrent UTIs
Low-dose vaginal estrogen significantly reduces UTI recurrence and should be proactively offered — AUA 2025 Grade B recommendation. Not offering this is a missed opportunity.
Sexual Desire / Libido
NICE 2024 and IMS recommend considering off-label transdermal testosterone when low sexual desire persists despite adequate systemic MHT. Discuss at every consultation — women rarely raise it.
Breast Cancer Survivors
Non-hormonal moisturisers and lubricants are first-line. Vaginal estrogen may be considered after oncologist discussion when non-hormonal measures fail (NICE 2024 updated recommendation).
⚠️
Long-term maintenance counseling: Counsel that GSM symptoms return on cessation of therapy, and long-term or indefinite maintenance may be needed — this is not dependency but a chronic condition management model.
📖 AUA/SUFU/AUGS GSM Guideline 2025 📖 ICSM 2024 📖 NICE NG23 2024 📖 NAMS 2020 GSM Position Statement

Domain 4 of 8

Sleep, Mood & Anxiety

Up to 60% of perimenopausal women experience clinically significant sleep disturbance. Mood, anxiety, and brain fog are frequently under-recognised and under-treated.

NAMS 2023 Non-Hormone Statement
NICE NG23 2024
WHO Mental Health Guidelines
Endocrine Society 2015
🔑
Evidence Principle — NAMS 2023 Non-pharmacological interventions including CBT, mindfulness, paced breathing, and clinical hypnosis have Level I evidence for hot flash bother and Level II evidence for mood and sleep improvement — these should be routinely offered.
CBT-I for Insomnia
CBT for insomnia (CBT-I) is the gold-standard first-line for sleep disorders. Sleep hygiene alone is insufficient for menopause-related insomnia — counsel this distinction explicitly to every patient.
MHT for Sleep
Where sleep disturbance is driven by night sweats, treating VMS directly (with MHT or menopause-specific CBT) improves sleep quality. Address the root cause, not just the sleep symptom.
Perimenopausal Low Mood
Estradiol (HRT) is effective for perimenopausal low mood that is not clinical depression. The Endocrine Society 2015 guideline and NAMS both support this use. SSRIs are not first-line for VMS.
Menopause-Related Anxiety
Counsel that anxiety is a recognised menopause symptom, not a personality change. Evidence supports MHT, CBT, and MBSR. Fezolinetant may also reduce anxiety concurrent with VMS (NAMS 2023).
Brain Fog / Cognitive Symptoms
Reassure: brain fog is real and neurologically plausible (reduced estrogen affects hippocampal and prefrontal function). Primary recommendation is lifestyle — aerobic exercise, sleep, stress reduction.
ICD-11 Recognition
WHO ICD-11 recognises GSM (code GA30.4) and menopausal mood symptoms as distinct clinical entities requiring active management — not simply reassurance. Code and treat accordingly.
📖 NAMS 2023 Non-Hormone Statement 📖 Endocrine Society 2015 📖 NICE NG23 📖 ICD-11 GA30
💙
Domain 5 of 8

Non-Hormonal Pharmacotherapy

For women who cannot or choose not to use MHT, NAMS 2023 and NICE 2024 provide evidence-tiered guidance. Set realistic expectations and individualise based on symptom profile.

NAMS 2023 Non-Hormone Statement
NICE NG23 2024
FDA PDUFA 2023
ACOG Practice Bulletin 141
⚠️
Critical Counseling Note — NICE 2024 Unregulated "bio-identical" compounded hormones sold outside regulated pharmacy systems have unknown efficacy and safety. Women must be clearly informed of this distinction vs. regulated pharmaceutical MHT.
Fezolinetant (NK3R Antagonist)
Non-hormonal, non-SSRI option for moderate-to-severe VMS where MHT is not appropriate. FDA-approved 2023; NAMS 2023 recommended. Onset within 1–2 weeks. Counsel on liver monitoring requirement.
SSRIs & SNRIs
Paroxetine salt (7.5mg) is FDA-approved for VMS. Escitalopram, venlafaxine, desvenlafaxine show VMS reduction. NICE 2024: SSRIs/SNRIs should NOT be first-line for VMS alone but are appropriate with concurrent depression/anxiety.
Gabapentin
Evidence for VMS reduction, particularly nocturnal hot flashes. Useful for women with comorbid neuropathic pain. Counsel on drowsiness, titration requirements, and misuse potential before prescribing.
Clonidine
Modest VMS effect. NICE 2024 explicitly states clonidine should NOT be offered routinely as first-line. Reserve for cases where all other options are not suitable — niche role only.
Oxybutynin
Evidence for hot flash reduction and bladder overactivity — useful in women with both GSM-urinary symptoms and VMS. An efficient dual-symptom option when both are present (NAMS 2023).
Herbal / Unregulated Preparations
Black cohosh, phytoestrogens, supplements: uncertain safety, unknown purity, limited quality evidence. Isoflavones may modestly help VMS. St John's Wort: significant drug interactions. NICE 2024 mandates informing patients (Rec 1.4.5–1.4.8).
📖 NAMS 2023 Non-Hormone Statement 📖 NICE NG23 2024 📖 FDA PDUFA 2023 📖 ACOG PB141

Domain 6 of 8

Lifestyle & Nutrition

All international guidelines are unanimous: lifestyle counseling is a mandatory component of every menopause consultation — not optional or supplementary.

FIGO Position Paper 2024
ICMR National Guidelines
WHO Physical Activity Recs
NAMS 2023 · Endocrine Society
Physical Activity
150 min/week moderate aerobic + 2 sessions strength training (ACOG, Endocrine Society, FIGO). High-impact loading (LIFTMOR protocol, jumping) proven to maintain or increase BMD. Resistance training prevents sarcopenia.
Calcium & Vitamin D
Calcium 1,200 mg/day (diet + supplement). Vitamin D 800–2,000 IU/day — Indian women often severely deficient per ICMR national data. These are not optional lifestyle tips but clinical requirements.
Mediterranean Diet
Reduces cardiovascular risk and is associated with reduced VMS severity. Reduce ultra-processed food, refined carbohydrates, and alcohol — all worsen VMS, sleep quality, and metabolic risk profile.
Weight Management
Visceral adiposity increases significantly after menopause due to estrogen withdrawal effects on fat distribution. A 5–10% weight reduction in overweight women reduces VMS frequency and improves metabolic markers.
Sleep Hygiene
Fixed sleep-wake times, cooler bedroom temperature, limiting alcohol and caffeine after 2pm, and avoiding screens 1 hour before bed — evidence-based and should be counseled at every consultation (NAMS 2023).
Smoking & Alcohol
Smoking: earlier menopause onset, more severe VMS, greater CVD and osteoporosis risk — cessation counseling is mandatory (WHO, ICMR). Alcohol >2 drinks/day increases breast cancer risk and worsens VMS burden.
🇮🇳
ICMR India Context (NFHS-5 data): Only 22% of Indian women aged 45–55 meet WHO physical activity guidelines. Vitamin D deficiency exceeds 70% in some Indian cohorts. Counseling must address these specific national gaps with practical, culturally adapted guidance.
📖 FIGO 2024 📖 ICMR Guidelines 📖 WHO Physical Activity Recommendations 📖 NFHS-5 2021 📖 NAMS 2023

Domain 7 of 8

Bone Health & Cardiovascular Risk

Bone loss of up to 10% occurs in the first 2–3 years around the FMP, and cardiovascular risk rises markedly without estrogen. Counseling must begin in perimenopause — not after a fracture.

NAMS 2022 HT Position
NAMS 2021 Osteoporosis Statement
IOF Guidelines · ACC/AHA
AUA/AUGS 2025
🔑
Window of Opportunity — NAMS 2022 Initiating MHT within 10 years of menopause onset or before age 60 maximises cardiovascular benefit and minimises harm. Counsel proactively rather than waiting for symptoms to become severe.
Bone Health Counseling
NAMS 2021 and IOF recommend counseling all women on fracture risk factors and calcium/Vitamin D adequacy. Introduce FRAX tool. MHT is a guideline-approved osteoporosis prevention option initiated in the window of opportunity.
Cardiovascular Risk
Estrogen withdrawal accelerates LDL rise, HDL fall, and triglyceride increase; weight redistributes centrally. For women under 60 with no CVD, early MHT initiation does not increase and may reduce cardiovascular risk (NAMS 2022, AHA).
Annual Monitoring
Annual blood pressure and fasting lipids are recommended during the transition by ACC/AHA and ACOG. Identify and manage hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and glucose dysregulation with specific plans — not just lifestyle advice.
Pelvic Floor Health
Estrogen deficiency weakens urethral support and pelvic musculature, worsening stress incontinence and prolapse. Pelvic floor physiotherapy (Kegel with proper technique) is primary intervention. Vaginal estrogen improves urethral mucosal health (AUA/AUGS 2025).
📖 NAMS 2022 HT Position 📖 NAMS 2021 Osteoporosis Statement 📖 IOF Guidelines 📖 ACC/AHA 📖 AUA/AUGS 2025

Domain 8 of 8

POI & Early Menopause

Menopause before 40 (POI) or 40–45 (early menopause) carries substantially higher cumulative CVD, osteoporosis, cognitive, and all-cause mortality risk. HRT is strongly recommended at least until ~age 51.

NICE NG23 2024
ESHRE POI Guideline
IMS White Paper 2024
ICMR Consensus on POI
🔑
NICE 2024 Explicit Statement POI and early menopause should be considered a continuum of elevated risk — bone, cognition, and cardiovascular impacts must be part of every counseling discussion, not deferred to later visits.
HRT in POI: Replacement Not Therapy
In POI, HRT is primarily hormone replacement — not a therapy for symptoms alone — and carries a fundamentally different risk-benefit profile from HRT in naturally menopausal women over 50. This distinction must be communicated clearly.
Psychological Support
The emotional and psychological impact of POI is profound — loss of fertility, altered body image, identity concerns. Referral to specialist psychological support (ideally a clinical psychologist with reproductive health expertise) is recommended (ESHRE).
Fertility Counseling
Spontaneous conception is possible in POI (5–10% of cases) — women must not be told conception is impossible. Refer to specialist fertility services early. Counsel on all family-building options including oocyte donation.
Long-Term Risk Counseling
Every POI counseling session must cover the elevated lifetime risk of cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, and cognitive decline. These must be active agenda items — not an afterthought — at every follow-up.
🇮🇳
ICMR / India Context: POI is underdiagnosed in Indian women, partly due to delayed presentation and attribution of symptoms to other causes. ICMR recommends FSH testing (two readings >25 IU/L, 4 weeks apart, with amenorrhoea ≥4 months) as the diagnostic standard, and emphasises MHT initiation without delay.
📖 NICE NG23 2024 📖 ESHRE POI Guideline 📖 IMS 2024 📖 ICMR Consensus on POI

Normal Antibodies

🛡️ What You're Seeing:

Green cells are your immune system's defenders - T-cells and white blood cells - patrolling throughout your body via the bloodstream.

They recognize harmful invaders (bacteria, viruses) while ignoring your own tissues. This is called "immune tolerance."

Notice how immune cells flow smoothly past organs without attacking them. This is healthy immunity at work!

🛡️

Defense System

Your immune system has millions of cells patrolling your body 24/7, identifying and destroying foreign invaders like bacteria, viruses, and cancer cells.

🔍

Recognition System

Immune cells learn to recognize "self" (your own cells) vs "non-self" (foreign invaders). This training happens in the thymus gland during childhood.

⚖️

Perfect Balance

Healthy immunity maintains perfect balance: strong enough to fight infections, but controlled enough not to attack your own body.

When Immune Tolerance Breaks Down

⚠️ What's Happening:

Red cells are immune cells that have lost the ability to recognize "self" - they now see your own tissues as threats.

This breakdown often starts with genetic susceptibility plus an environmental trigger (infection, stress, hormones).

Watch as confused immune cells begin to circle and target healthy organs instead of just patrolling for invaders.

Genetic Factors

  • HLA genes control immune recognition
  • Certain gene variants increase risk 5-10x
  • Women have 2 X chromosomes = more immune genes
  • Family history significantly increases risk
🌍

Environmental Triggers

  • Infections: Viruses can trigger molecular mimicry
  • Stress: Chronic stress weakens immune regulation
  • Hormones: Estrogen enhances immune responses
  • Toxins: Chemicals may alter cell surface markers
  • UV Light: Sun exposure can trigger lupus flares

💡 Molecular Mimicry Explained

Sometimes, proteins on bacteria or viruses look similar to your own body's proteins. When immune cells learn to attack the infection, they accidentally learn to attack your own cells too.

Self-Attack: The Autoimmune Battle

💥 The Attack in Action:

Red and orange cells are attacking immune cells - T-cells directly destroying tissue, and B-cells releasing autoantibodies.

Yellow bursts represent inflammation - chemicals released that cause pain, swelling, heat, and redness.

Notice the concentrated attacks on specific organs: joints in RA, thyroid in Hashimoto's, multiple organs in Lupus.

Blue waves are cytokines - messenger proteins that amplify the attack and recruit more immune cells to join.

The Attack Cascade

Stage 1 Recognition Failure

T-cells fail to recognize "self" markers on healthy cells. The tolerance mechanism breaks down.

⚠️ Point of No Return
Stage 2 Autoantibody Production

B-cells start producing antibodies against your own proteins. These autoantibodies circulate in the blood.

Examples: Anti-nuclear antibodies (ANA) in lupus, Anti-CCP in rheumatoid arthritis
Stage 3 Tissue Destruction

Immune cells and antibodies attack healthy tissues. Inflammation damages organs.

🔥 Joints: Cartilage erosion (RA)
🦋 Skin: Rashes, lesions (Lupus)
🫀 Organs: Kidney damage (Lupus nephritis)
Stage 4 Chronic Inflammation

Continuous immune activation creates persistent inflammation. This leads to progressive organ damage over time.

Fatigue Pain Dysfunction Scarring

Mental Health

Mental Health & Depression Management

Critical mental health considerations for autoimmune disease patients

Depression Prevalence

ACOG 2021 data: 25-60% of autoimmune patients experience clinical depression - 2-3x higher than general population.

Common causes: Chronic inflammation (cytokine effects on brain), disease burden, medication side effects (corticosteroids), lifestyle limitations.

Screening Tools (WHO)

Regular screening recommended using validated tools:

  • PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire) - every 6 months
  • GAD-7 for anxiety assessment
  • Fatigue Severity Scale (FSS)

Treatment Approach

Evidence-based interventions:

• SSRIs: First-line (escitalopram, sertraline) - safe with most immunosuppressants
• CBT: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy - 60% response rate
• Exercise: 30 min moderate activity 5×/week reduces depression 47% (meta-analysis, 2020)
• Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR): ACR-endorsed

Warning Signs - Seek Immediate Help

Suicidal thoughts, self-harm urges, severe hopelessness, inability to function. Contact mental health crisis line 24/7: KIRAN 1800-599-0019 (India) | National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 988 (USA)

Immunization

Vaccination Guidelines

WHO/CDC/ACIP recommendations for immunocompromised patients (Updated 2025)

Essential Vaccines for Autoimmune Patients

Influenza (Annual)

Recommended: Inactivated vaccine only (NOT live attenuated)
Timing: Ideally BEFORE starting immunosuppression
Evidence: Reduces severe flu by 70% in SLE patients (ACR 2023)

COVID-19 (Current)

Recommended: mRNA vaccines (Pfizer/Moderna) preferred
Timing: Primary series + annual booster
Special: May need extra dose if on rituximab, cyclophosphamide

Pneumococcal

Recommended: PCV20 (single dose) OR PCV15 + PPSV23
Timing: Before starting immunosuppression if possible
Revaccination: PPSV23 after 5 years if high-risk

Shingles (Herpes Zoster)

Recommended: Shingrix (recombinant) - 2 doses
Age: ≥50 years OR ≥19 if immunocompromised
Risk: Autoimmune patients have 2-10x higher shingles risk

HPV (If ≤26 years)

Recommended: 9-valent HPV vaccine (Gardasil 9)
Reason: ↑ cervical cancer risk with immunosuppression
Timing: Complete series before starting therapy

Hepatitis B

Recommended: 3-dose series (especially if rituximab planned)
Check: Anti-HBs titers after vaccination
Note: May need higher doses or additional doses

Contraindicated: Live Vaccines

AVOID if on immunosuppression: MMR, Varicella (chickenpox), Oral polio, Live flu (FluMist), Yellow fever, Oral typhoid, BCG.
Exception: Can give if immunosuppression stopped ≥3 months AND immune function recovered (consult rheumatologist).

Timing guidance (ACIP): Ideally vaccinate ≥2-4 weeks BEFORE starting immunosuppressive therapy. If already on therapy, vaccines are still beneficial but may have reduced efficacy. Household contacts should also be vaccinated to create "cocooning" protection.

Pandemic Preparedness

COVID-19 & Autoimmune Disease

Updated guidance for immunocompromised patients (WHO/CDC 2025)

Risk Stratification

Higher Risk Patients:
  • On rituximab, cyclophosphamide, high-dose corticosteroids (>20mg prednisone)
  • Active organ involvement (lupus nephritis, myocarditis)
  • Poorly controlled disease
  • Multiple comorbidities (diabetes, obesity, CKD)
Lower Risk Patients:
  • Well-controlled disease on stable therapy
  • On hydroxychloroquine alone or TNF inhibitors
  • Younger age, no comorbidities

Treatment Options (If Infected)

Paxlovid (Nirmatrelvir/Ritonavir)

Indication: High-risk within 5 days of symptoms
Caution: Drug interactions with MTX, tacrolimus - adjust doses

Molnupiravir

Alternative: If Paxlovid contraindicated
Efficacy: 30% reduction in hospitalization

Remdesivir (Veklury)

For: Hospitalized patients with pneumonia
Duration: 3-5 day IV course

Medication Continuity

Generally CONTINUE:
  • Hydroxychloroquine (may be protective)
  • TNF inhibitors, IL-6 inhibitors
  • Low-dose corticosteroids (<10mg prednisone)
Consider HOLDING (consult rheumatologist):
  • Methotrexate, azathioprine, mycophenolate
  • Rituximab (delay next dose)
  • JAK inhibitors
Pregnancy Critical

Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS)

Critical autoimmune condition causing blood clots & pregnancy complications - ACOG/ACR Guidelines 2024

Diagnostic Criteria

Requires: ≥1 clinical criterion + ≥1 laboratory criterion (confirmed 12 weeks apart)

Clinical:
  • ≥1 unexplained thrombosis (arterial or venous)
  • ≥3 consecutive miscarriages (<10 weeks)
  • ≥1 fetal death (≥10 weeks) with normal anatomy
  • Premature birth (<34 weeks) due to preeclampsia/placental insufficiency
Laboratory:
  • Lupus anticoagulant (LA) positive
  • Anticardiolipin IgG/IgM (>40 GPL/MPL units)
  • Anti-β2 glycoprotein-I IgG/IgM (>99th percentile)

Pregnancy Management

Standard Treatment (ACOG):

• Aspirin 81mg daily - start preconception
• Heparin/LMWH throughout pregnancy + 6 weeks postpartum
• Enoxaparin 40mg SC daily or unfractionated heparin

Monitoring:

Weekly NSTs from 32 weeks, growth scans every 3-4 weeks, close BP monitoring (preeclampsia risk 50%)

Catastrophic APS (CAPS) - Medical Emergency

Presentation: Rapid multi-organ thrombosis (within 1 week). Affects 1% of APS patients. Mortality 30-50%. Treatment: IV heparin, high-dose corticosteroids, plasmapheresis, IVIG. Requires ICU admission.

Key Point: 30-40% of SLE patients have antiphospholipid antibodies, but only ~10% develop APS. Triple-positive antibody status (LA + aCL + anti-β2GPI) carries highest risk. Women with APS should delay pregnancy until disease is stable ≥6 months, consult maternal-fetal medicine specialist pre-conception.

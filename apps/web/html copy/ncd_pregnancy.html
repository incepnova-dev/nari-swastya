Understanding NCDs in Pregnancy

Hover over each card to reveal key management strategies. Click for detailed information from WHO, ACOG, and ICMR guidelines.

10-20%
🩸

Diabetes & GDM

Affects 10-20% of pregnancies in India. Early screening and management crucial for healthy outcomes.

Key Management Points

  • Target glucose: Fasting 70-95 mg/dL
  • Monitor 4-7 times daily
  • Medical nutrition therapy first
  • Insulin safe if needed
  • Post-meal walking reduces spikes 20-30%
  • 50% risk Type 2 diabetes in 10 years
10-15%
💙

Hypertension

Blood pressure monitoring and preeclampsia prevention with evidence-based strategies.

Prevention & Management

  • Low-dose aspirin from 12 weeks
  • Target BP: 130-150/80-100 mmHg
  • Home monitoring twice daily
  • DASH diet: low sodium, high K+
  • Safe medications: labetalol, nifedipine
  • Call 108 if BP >160/110
2-5%
🦋

Thyroid Disorders

Critical for fetal brain development. Medication adjustments needed throughout pregnancy.

Monitoring & Treatment

  • Target TSH <2.5 (1st trimester)
  • Dose increases 30-50%
  • Check TSH every 4 weeks
  • Take on empty stomach
  • Levothyroxine completely safe
  • Critical for baby's brain development
50-60%
🩸

Anemia

Most common nutritional disorder in Indian pregnancy. Iron supplementation essential.

Treatment Guidelines

  • Target Hb >11 g/dL
  • Iron 100-200mg daily
  • Take with vitamin C
  • Avoid tea/coffee within 1 hour
  • IV iron if Hb <9 in 3rd trimester
  • Continue 3-6 months postpartum
Risk↑
⚖️

Weight Management

BMI optimization critical. Healthy weight gain targets prevent complications.

Weight Gain Goals

  • Normal BMI: 11.5-16 kg
  • Overweight: 7-11.5 kg
  • Obese: 5-9 kg
  • Exercise 150 min/week
  • Focus on nutrition quality
  • Gradual loss postpartum
1-4%
❤️

Cardiac Conditions

Requires multidisciplinary care. Planning essential for optimal outcomes.

Special Considerations

  • Cardiology + OB co-management
  • Vaginal delivery preferred
  • Fluid management critical
  • Risk stratification (WHO I-IV)
  • Medication optimization
  • Delivery at specialized center

🛡️ Prevention First

Precautions to Prevent NCDs

Proactive steps you can take BEFORE and DURING pregnancy to reduce NCD risk by 30-50% and optimize outcomes for you and your baby.

🏃‍♀️

Physical Activity

  • 30-40 minutes moderate exercise most days reduces GDM risk by 30-40%
  • Walking after meals lowers postprandial glucose by 25%
  • Continue throughout pregnancy unless contraindicated
  • Swimming, prenatal yoga, stationary cycling are safe
  • Pelvic floor exercises prevent incontinence
🥗

Optimal Nutrition

  • Balanced diet with whole grains, vegetables, lean proteins
  • Limit refined carbs and sugary foods
  • Increase fiber intake (25-30g daily)
  • Omega-3 fatty acids for fetal brain development
  • Folic acid 400-800mcg daily pre-conception
  • Adequate hydration (8-10 glasses water daily)
⚖️

Weight Optimization

  • Achieve BMI <25 kg/m² before conceiving
  • Each BMI unit decrease = 10% lower GDM risk
  • Gradual weight loss safer than crash diets
  • Maintain healthy weight between pregnancies
  • 18-24 month interpregnancy interval optimal
🚭

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Stop smoking—increases all NCD risks
  • Limit alcohol consumption completely
  • Manage stress through yoga, meditation, counseling
  • Adequate sleep (7-9 hours)—<7 hours doubles GDM risk
  • Avoid environmental toxins and pollutants
💊

Pre-Conception Planning

  • Folic acid supplementation 3 months before conception
  • Iron stores optimization (ferritin >30 ng/mL)
  • Thyroid screening if risk factors present
  • Glucose testing if overweight or family history
  • Update vaccinations (rubella, varicella)
  • Review all medications for pregnancy safety
🩺

Early Prenatal Care

  • First visit before 12 weeks ideally
  • Baseline labs: CBC, glucose, TSH, BP
  • Risk assessment for NCDs
  • Early screening if high-risk (don't wait 24-28 weeks)
  • Regular monitoring schedule adherence
  • Low-dose aspirin if preeclampsia risk
👨‍👩‍👧

Family Support

  • Educate family about NCDs and prevention
  • Involve partner in lifestyle changes
  • Create supportive home environment
  • Healthy cooking and meal planning together
  • Social support reduces complications by 30-40%
📱

Technology & Tracking

  • Use glucose meter if GDM—log all readings
  • Home BP monitor for hypertension
  • Pregnancy apps for weight, nutrition tracking
  • Set medication reminders
  • Keep digital copies of medical records
💬 Empowered Conversations

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

Being prepared with the right questions ensures you get personalized, comprehensive care. Print this list and bring it to your appointments for maximum benefit.

🩸 About Diabetes & GDM

  • What is my specific risk for gestational diabetes based on my history?
  • Should I be screened earlier than 24-28 weeks given my risk factors?
  • What are my target blood glucose levels during pregnancy?
  • How often should I check my blood sugar if diagnosed with GDM?
  • Can I control GDM with diet alone or will I need medication?
  • Is metformin or insulin safer for my situation?
  • What foods should I completely avoid or limit?
  • How will my GDM affect the timing and mode of delivery?
  • What is my risk of developing Type 2 diabetes after delivery?
  • When should I get tested for diabetes after giving birth?

💙 About Blood Pressure & Preeclampsia

  • What is my baseline blood pressure and what should my target be?
  • Am I at high risk for preeclampsia based on my medical history?
  • Should I take low-dose aspirin? If yes, when should I start?
  • Should I monitor my blood pressure at home? How often?
  • Which blood pressure medications are safe during pregnancy?
  • What symptoms should prompt me to call you immediately?
  • Will I need more frequent ultrasounds to monitor the baby?
  • How will hypertension affect my delivery timing and plan?
  • What is my long-term cardiovascular risk after preeclampsia?
  • Can I breastfeed while on blood pressure medication?

🦋 About Thyroid Function

  • What is my current TSH level and what should it be in pregnancy?
  • How often will my thyroid levels be checked?
  • Will my levothyroxine dose need to be increased?
  • What time of day should I take my thyroid medication?
  • Can my thyroid medication interact with prenatal vitamins?
  • How will untreated hypothyroidism affect my baby?
  • Is my thyroid condition genetic? Will my baby be at risk?
  • Will my medication dose change after delivery?
  • Should I be screened for postpartum thyroiditis?

🩸 About Anemia & Iron Deficiency

  • What is my current hemoglobin level? Is it adequate for pregnancy?
  • What type of anemia do I have? (iron deficiency, B12, folate)
  • How much iron should I be taking daily?
  • Should I take iron separately from my prenatal vitamin?
  • What foods can help boost my iron absorption?
  • Will I need IV iron? At what hemoglobin level?
  • How often should my hemoglobin be rechecked?
  • What is the minimum safe hemoglobin level for delivery?
  • Should I continue iron supplementation after delivery?

💊 About Medications & Safety

  • Which of my regular medications are safe to continue during pregnancy?
  • Do any of my medications need to be changed or stopped?
  • What pain relievers can I safely take during pregnancy?
  • Are there any over-the-counter medications I should avoid?
  • Can I take herbal supplements or traditional remedies?
  • What medications are safe while breastfeeding?
  • How do I manage medication side effects during pregnancy?
  • Should I have a medication emergency plan?

🏥 About Delivery Planning

  • Will my NCD affect when I should deliver? (Earlier or at term?)
  • Should I plan for induction or wait for spontaneous labor?
  • Will I need to deliver at a hospital with a higher level NICU?
  • What is the likelihood I'll need a cesarean delivery?
  • Will I have continuous fetal monitoring during labor?
  • Should any specialists be present at my delivery?
  • What special monitoring will my baby need after birth?
  • How long can I expect to stay in the hospital postpartum?

🤱 Critical for NCDs

Breastfeeding with NCDs

Breastfeeding is one of the most powerful protective factors. Evidence shows 47% reduction in diabetes risk, 30% lower cardiovascular disease, and dramatic health benefits for babies.

💖

Reduces YOUR Diabetes Risk

Breastfeeding 6+ months reduces mother's Type 2 diabetes risk by 47%. For GDM women, exclusive breastfeeding cuts progression to diabetes by 50%.

47% Risk Reduction

Source: WHO, JAMA 2020

❤️

Cardiovascular Protection

Each month of breastfeeding lowers CVD risk by 1-2%. Women breastfeeding 12+ months have 11% lower hypertension and 14% lower heart disease risk.

30% Lower CVD Risk

Source: ACOG, Circulation 2019

🩸

Blood Pressure Control

Breastfeeding mothers have lower BP both during lactation and long-term. Oxytocin release has natural BP-lowering effects.

5-10 mmHg ↓

Source: ICMR 2024

👶

Baby's Metabolic Health

Breastfed babies have 15-30% lower obesity risk and 30% lower Type 2 diabetes in adulthood. Each additional month provides incremental protection.

30% Lower Obesity

Source: WHO, Pediatrics 2021

🧠

Cognitive Development

Babies of diabetic mothers who breastfeed show better neurodevelopmental outcomes. Optimal nutrition for brain development.

+3-5 IQ Points

Source: Lancet 2020

⚖️

Postpartum Weight Loss

Burns 300-500 extra calories daily. Mothers exclusively breastfeeding 6 months lose significantly more weight and return to pre-pregnancy weight faster.

500 Cal/Day Burn

Source: ACOG 2023

Comparative Risk: WITH vs WITHOUT Breastfeeding

✅ WITH Breastfeeding

Maternal Diabetes: 50% lower GDM→Type 2 progression

Maternal Heart: 30% lower CVD risk long-term

Baby Infections: 50% fewer ear, 70% fewer GI infections

SIDS Risk: 36% lower risk

⚠️ WITHOUT Breastfeeding

Maternal Diabetes: 2x higher Type 2 risk within 10 years

Maternal Heart: Elevated CVD risk, especially with GDM

Baby Infections: Double respiratory & GI infection rates

Baby Obesity: 15-30% higher childhood/adult obesity

WHO Recommendation: Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months, continued with complementary foods up to 2 years. ESPECIALLY critical for NCD mothers.

💎 Essential Knowledge

Must-Know Facts for Women & Families

Critical insights that every woman and family should understand about NCDs during pregnancy. Click each card to explore detailed information.

Metabolic
📊

Insulin Resistance Peaks

Critical Period
Insulin resistance peaks at 26-33 weeks of pregnancy due to placental hormones, not dietary failure.
What This Means
Glucose levels that were controlled earlier may suddenly worsen even without dietary changes. This is normal physiology.
Action Required
Insulin doses often need to be increased during this period. Don't feel discouraged—it's expected and temporary.
+
Safety Alert
🌙

Night-time Hypoglycemia Risk

Why It's Dangerous
Night-time low blood sugar is more dangerous than daytime episodes because you may not wake up or recognize symptoms while sleeping.
Essential Precautions
Always have a bedtime snack with protein and complex carbohydrates.
Emergency Preparedness
Keep glucose tablets on your nightstand within easy reach.
+
Critical
🦋

Thyroid Medication Needs

Early Increase Required
Thyroid medication needs increase by 30-50% as early as 4-6 weeks of pregnancy.
Immediate Action
TSH should be checked as soon as pregnancy is confirmed.
Critical Window
Untreated hypothyroidism in early pregnancy can affect fetal brain development (weeks 8-12) even if you feel fine.
+
High Risk
🔄

PCOS & GDM Connection

Prevalence in India
PCOS affects 10-15% of Indian women.
GDM Risk
40-50% of women with PCOS will develop gestational diabetes.
Early Screening
If you have PCOS, request glucose screening in the first trimester rather than waiting until 24-28 weeks.
+
Postpartum
🦋

Postpartum Thyroiditis

Occurrence Rate
Affects 5-10% of women in the first year after delivery.
Symptom Pattern
Causes hyperthyroidism followed by hypothyroidism. Watch for anxiety, palpitations, or extreme fatigue postpartum.
Misdiagnosis Risk
Often misdiagnosed as postpartum depression. Request thyroid testing if symptoms occur.
+
Pre-Pregnancy
💊

Statins & Pregnancy

Stop Before Conception
Statins must be stopped 3 months before attempting pregnancy as they can cause birth defects.
Good News
High cholesterol itself doesn't harm the baby during pregnancy. Focus on diet and exercise.
After Delivery
Statins can be restarted after delivery if not breastfeeding.
+
Critical Switch
⚠️

Blood Pressure Medications

Dangerous Medications
ACE inhibitors and ARBs cause severe fetal kidney damage if taken in second/third trimester.
Must Switch Before Pregnancy
Change medications BEFORE getting pregnant, not after.
Safe Alternatives
Methyldopa, labetalol, and nifedipine are safe throughout pregnancy and breastfeeding.
+
Treatment Option
💊

Metformin in Pregnancy

Safety Profile
Metformin crosses the placenta but is considered safe during pregnancy.
PCOS Benefits
May reduce miscarriage risk in PCOS and lower gestational weight gain compared to insulin alone.
International Acceptance
Some guidelines now accept it as first-line for GDM, though insulin remains the gold standard in India.
+
Prevention
🩹

Aspirin for Preeclampsia

Critical Timing
Start aspirin (75-150 mg) by 12-16 weeks at the latest. Starting after 16 weeks is significantly less effective.
Best Practice
Take at bedtime for maximum benefit.
Safety Assurance
Does NOT increase bleeding risk at delivery at this low dose.
+
Temporary Effect
💉

Steroids & Blood Sugar

Purpose
Steroid injections for fetal lung maturity are crucial for preterm babies.
Glucose Impact
Cause significant blood sugar spikes requiring glucose monitoring for 48-72 hours.
Insulin Adjustment
Insulin needs may temporarily double or triple. This is expected and doses will decrease after the steroid effect wears off.
+
Hidden Danger
🔍

"Silent" Preeclampsia

HELLP Syndrome
Can develop with normal blood pressure. Affects liver and platelets.
Warning Signs
Severe upper abdominal pain, nausea/vomiting in third trimester, or sudden swelling.
Action Required
Seek immediate evaluation even if BP readings are normal.
+
Vascular Risk
🔴

Placental Problems

Increased Risk
Abruption and insufficiency are 2-3 times more common with chronic hypertension and diabetes.
Cause
Due to blood vessel damage from chronic conditions.
Why Surveillance Matters
Third-trimester monitoring (NSTs, ultrasounds) is critical—these complications can develop suddenly.
+
Glucose Signal
💧

Polyhydramnios Warning

What It Signals
Excess amniotic fluid in diabetic pregnancy indicates poor glucose control.
Associated Risks
Increases risk of preterm labor and postpartum hemorrhage.
Immediate Action
Glucose control must be intensified immediately with more frequent monitoring.
+
Infection Risk
🦠

UTIs & Diabetes

Higher Frequency & Danger
UTIs are more common and more dangerous with diabetes.
Progression Risk
25% develop kidney infections if untreated.
Prevention
Report burning urination immediately. Monthly urine testing recommended as many diabetic women have asymptomatic infections.
+
Myth Buster
❌

GDM & Miscarriage Myth

Important Truth
Gestational diabetes does NOT cause first-trimester miscarriages—that's a myth.
Pre-existing Diabetes
However, pre-existing diabetes with poor control (HbA1c >8%) increases miscarriage risk 2-3 fold.
Key Message
Pre-conception glucose control is vital for women with pre-existing diabetes.
+
Newborn Care
👶

Mineral Imbalances in Babies

Risk Period
Babies of diabetic mothers are at risk for low calcium and magnesium in the first 24-48 hours after birth.
Symptoms
May cause jitteriness or seizures.
Routine Screening
Your baby will need blood tests after delivery. This is routine screening, not a sign something is wrong.
+
Monitoring
🤰

Fetal Movement Patterns

Normal vs. Concerning
Fetal hiccups are normal and NOT a sign of distress.
Red Flags
Sudden increase in fetal movement or prolonged decreased movement (after 28 weeks) requires same-day evaluation.
Tracking Tool
Download a kick-count app. Aim for 10 movements in 2 hours when baby is usually active.
+
Delivery Timing
📅

Respiratory Distress Risk

Risk Factor
Respiratory distress syndrome risk increases if cesarean is performed before 39 weeks without labor.
Applies to All
This applies even in babies of diabetic mothers.
Recommendation
Elective cesareans should wait until 39 weeks unless there's a medical indication for earlier delivery.
+
Common & Treatable
☀️

Neonatal Jaundice

Higher Incidence
More common in babies born to mothers with diabetes or blood group incompatibility.
Treatment
Requires phototherapy monitoring. Easily treatable.
Reassurance
Doesn't cause long-term problems if caught early with regular pediatric follow-up.
+
Technology
📱

Continuous Glucose Monitors

Availability in India
CGMs are increasingly available and show real-time glucose trends.
Benefits
Reduce finger-prick testing and dramatically improve glucose control by revealing hidden patterns after meals.
Cost Consideration
₹4,000-8,000 for 14 days. While expensive, can be valuable for difficult-to-control diabetes.
+
Nutrition Tip
🍽️

Artificial Sweeteners Safety

Safe Options
Sucralose and stevia are safe during pregnancy in moderate amounts.
Avoid If PKU
Aspartame should be avoided if you have phenylketonuria (PKU).
Natural Alternatives
Dates and jaggery still raise blood sugar and must be counted in carbohydrate intake.
+
Smart Eating
🥗

Meal Sequencing Matters

Proven Strategy
Eating vegetables and protein BEFORE carbohydrates can reduce post-meal glucose spikes by 25-40%.
How to Apply
At each meal: 1) Eat salad/vegetables first 2) Then protein 3) Finally rice/roti last.
Simple & Effective
No special foods needed—just change the order you eat.
+
Sleep & Stress
😴

Stress Impacts Glucose

Hormonal Effect
Stress and poor sleep directly raise blood glucose through cortisol and other stress hormones.
Quantifiable Impact
Can add 20-40 mg/dL to your glucose readings.
Management Strategy
Prioritize 7-8 hours of sleep and practice stress reduction (meditation, prenatal yoga, breathing exercises) as part of glucose management.
+
Intimacy
💑

Sexual Activity Safety

Generally Safe
Sexual activity is safe with most NCDs unless specifically contraindicated.
Contraindications
Avoid if: placenta previa, preterm labor risk, or ruptured membranes.
Reassurance
Does NOT cause preterm labor in healthy pregnancies. Can continue until delivery if comfortable.
+
Critical Window
⏰

GDM & Future Diabetes Risk

Stark Statistics
Women with GDM have 10 times higher risk of developing Type 2 diabetes within 10 years.
Powerful Prevention
Lifestyle changes (7% weight loss, 150 min/week exercise) reduce this risk by 58%.
Your Opportunity
The postpartum period is your window to prevent lifelong diabetes—use it!
+
Long-term Protection
🤱

Breastfeeding Reduces Diabetes Risk

Metabolic Benefits
Breastfeeding for 6+ months reduces your lifetime diabetes risk by 25-30%.
Beyond Weight Loss
Protection comes from metabolic benefits independent of weight loss.
Dose-Response Effect
Each additional month of breastfeeding provides incremental protection. Aim for at least one year if possible.
+

🩸 Gestational Diabetes Management

📊
Universal Screening Recommended

DIPSI (Diabetes in Pregnancy Study Group India) criteria used for diagnosis. All pregnant women should be screened at 24-28 weeks.

🍽️
Medical Nutrition Therapy First-Line

Start with diet modification and lifestyle changes. Progress to medication only if targets not met within 2 weeks.

💉
Insulin Preferred, Metformin Acceptable

Insulin remains gold standard in India, though metformin is increasingly accepted for selected cases.

💓 Hypertension in Pregnancy

🩹
Low-Dose Aspirin Prevention

Start from 12 weeks for high-risk women to prevent preeclampsia. Timing is critical for effectiveness.

📅
Delivery Timing Based on Severity

Individualized approach based on blood pressure control, maternal condition, and fetal well-being.

🔄
Postpartum Follow-up Essential

Monitor for chronic hypertension risk. Women with gestational hypertension need long-term cardiovascular assessment.

🥗 Nutrition Guidelines

🌾
Diverse Diet with Local Foods

Emphasis on culturally appropriate, locally available foods. No need for expensive imported supplements.

💊
Iron and Folic Acid for All

Universal supplementation recommended given high anemia prevalence in Indian women.

🦴
Calcium Supplementation

Recommended in calcium-deficient populations to prevent preeclampsia and support fetal bone development.

👶 Antenatal Care Model

8️⃣
Minimum 8 Contacts (Updated 2016)

Increased from 4 visits to ensure comprehensive care. First contact should be by 12 weeks.

😊
Positive Pregnancy Experience Focus

Shift from solely risk-detection to promoting positive experience with respect, dignity, and emotional support.

🤝
Partner and Family Involvement

Encourage family participation in care decisions and birth planning for better outcomes.

🏥 NCD Management Integration

🔗
Integration into Maternal Health Services

NCDs should be screened and managed as part of routine antenatal care, not as separate programs.

👩‍⚕️
Task-Shifting to Midwives and Nurses

Trained midwives and nurses can manage uncomplicated NCDs with physician backup.

🏘️
Community-Based Care Where Possible

Bring services closer to women through community health workers and local health centers.

🩺 Diabetes Management (ACOG)

💑
Pre-Conception Counseling Essential

All women with diabetes planning pregnancy should receive comprehensive counseling and optimization.

📊
HbA1c Target Before Conception

<6.5% recommended before attempting pregnancy to minimize congenital anomaly risk.

🩹
Aspirin for Preeclampsia Prevention

Low-dose aspirin (81mg) from 12-16 weeks for women with diabetes and other risk factors.

📅
Delivery Timing Guidelines

Well-controlled: 39-39+6 weeks. With complications: individualized, may be earlier based on maternal-fetal status.

⚖️ Obesity Management

⚠️
Pregnancy NOT Time for Weight Loss

Focus on healthy eating and appropriate weight gain, not weight reduction during pregnancy.

📈
Gestational Weight Gain Targets

Based on pre-pregnancy BMI: Normal (11.5-16kg), Overweight (7-11.5kg), Obese (5-9kg).

🔄
Inter-Conception Period Intervention

Address obesity between pregnancies for better outcomes in future pregnancies.

🏥 Mode of Delivery Comparison

🤰

Vaginal Delivery

  • Preferred option if no obstetric contraindications exist
  • Faster recovery and shorter hospital stay
  • Lower infection risk compared to cesarean
  • Better for future pregnancies (no scar complications)
  • Natural hormonal benefits for mother and baby
  • Induction considerations: Cervical ripening may be needed, success rates lower with obesity
  • Continuous fetal monitoring recommended for diabetes and hypertension
⚕️

Cesarean Section

  • Obstetric indications: Failed induction, cephalopelvic disproportion, fetal distress, malpresentation
  • Medical indications: Severe preeclampsia, proliferative retinopathy, cardiovascular compromise
  • Increased risks with NCDs: wound infection, DVT, poor wound healing (diabetes)
  • Anesthetic complications more common with obesity
  • Longer recovery period and hospital stay
  • Requires experienced surgical and anesthesia teams
  • Future pregnancy considerations (scar assessment needed)

⚡ Labor Management for NCDs

📊 Continuous Fetal Monitoring Required for women with diabetes and hypertension to detect fetal distress early
💉 IV Glucose-Insulin Infusion For diabetic women during labor - maintain glucose 70-110 mg/dL
🩸 Active Third Stage Management Reduces postpartum hemorrhage risk, especially important with diabetes and hypertension

📅 Delivery Timing Guidelines

✅
Well-Controlled Conditions
  • 🍽️ GDM (diet only): 40 weeks
  • 💊 GDM (on medication): 39-39+6 weeks
  • 🩺 Chronic HTN (controlled): 38-39 weeks
⚠️
Pre-existing Conditions
  • 💉 Pre-existing diabetes (well-controlled): 37-39 weeks
  • 📊 Based on maternal-fetal assessment
  • 🔍 Close monitoring required
🚨
Emergency Situations
  • ⚡ Severe preeclampsia: Immediate delivery once stabilized
  • 👶 IUGR: 32-37 weeks based on Doppler
  • 🏥 Regardless of gestational age if indicated

⚖️ Balancing Act: Key Principle

Delivery timing must balance the risk of continuing pregnancy against the risk of prematurity. Each decision is individualized based on NCD control, maternal condition, fetal well-being, and gestational age.
